Intel recently announced a plan to eliminate 15,000 jobs after historically bad quarterly results.

It’s been rehearsed to death that Intel missed the boat on mobile and AI, but IMHO they should have heard an equally serious alarm bell when Apple ditched Intel for its own processors in 2020 (which then glowing reviews). In the scheme of things, Apple is a small vendor, but the problem for Intel is that their move seems to have demonstrated companies that ARM-based laptops are viable, and that’s a problem. This should be Intel’s core competency.

Moreover Intel’s desktop chips are having serious stability issues; its next generation of chips is expected to have unusually high production costs; and AMD is gaining ground in both the server and desktop markets. It’s a bad time to be Intel, but in some ways this is a classic case of an old story: Intel didn’t want to compete against its own profit centers by investing in alternatives, and now that the market has shifted, Intel has fallen behind.

But the thing is, from what I can see, AMD is not exactly hitting it out of the park. I started paying attention to this when I looked into buying a mini-PC in early 2024. I wanted something approximately the size and performance of a Playstation 5 but with a bit more flexibility. My thought was that with the PS5 released nearly 4 years ago, and with processors expected to double in performance every 18 months, I could find find something that met the bill.

In January 2024, AMD released two processors, the 8700G and the 8600G, that looked well-suited for this on paper. They’re both APUs (“Accelerated Processing Unit” — who comes up with these names?) with CPUs and GPUs on a single die, that are apparently meant to serve the low-power home PC gaming market. But they both got pretty tepid reviews because their price propositions don’t work.1 Then, In July, AMD released a laptop processor that outperformsthose two processors handily with lower power consumption.

So what exactly was AMD thinking when it released the 8700G and 8600G? Why did it release overpriced, underperforming products and then make them obsolete 6 months later?

One explanation is that big companies are like octopuses, and each product division/armn is essentially its own brain with its own reward structures. I think this model works pretty well for Google, where product shutdowns are depressingly regular (people set up websites to count down to cancellation) and generally attributed to misaligned incentives. But AMD’s issues seem to be less about multiple aims and more about about inability to execute. The strategy of low-power, integrated chips cutting away at Nvidia’s market share at the low end was and is sensible. But the details need to work, and they often don’t.

Some other places where AMD’s displays a somewhat baffling inattention to detail:

  • Its naming scheme for its laptop parts is truly a marvel of impossible to remember details. Why make this hard for buyers? (This is a surprisingly common problem among manufacturers — what does “G34WQi” evoke for you?)

  • Its most recent flagship desktop processor is getting mixed to negative reviews because of unexplained performance issues, as well as a serious driver issue that’s lasted for ~16 months

  • The company continues to lag badly in the AI/GPU market in large part because its software isn’t competitive. (Lisa Su says, about her experiences with the Playstation 3: “Perhaps one could say, if you look in hindsight, programmability is so important.” Ok but so how about today…)

Basically, I see a company that capitalized on its main competitor’s struggles and continues to release some very successful, specialized products, but that seems to be its own worst enemy in terms of execution and launch. I think this has gotten less attention than it might in light of Intel’s more obviously existential struggle. But inability to execute is also a crisis, albeit one that moves more slowly.

If I were tasked with fixing things at AMD, I would say:

  • Get the product marketing right. Name the products simple, memorable things. Look at Apple.

  • If the price proposition doesn’t work, subsidize. Sell the 8600G at a loss and make future processors backwards compatible and encourage upgrades.

  • Make the AI software work.

But who knows? I am just seeing a small part of the puzzle. I will continue to watch with interest.
